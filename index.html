The Dawn of Counting

The Ishango Bone: Africa's 20,000-Year-Old Calculator ü¶¥

Long before Pythagoras, long before Babylon, there was the Ishango bone - discovered in 1960 near Lake Edward in the Democratic Republic of Congo. This baboon fibula, carved with deliberate notches arranged in three columns, dates back approximately 20,000-25,000 years, making it potentially the oldest mathematical artifact in human history!

What makes it mind-blowing:

  • The left column shows the prime numbers between 10 and 20: 11, 13, 17, 19
  • One column appears to show doubling patterns
  • The numbers suggest knowledge of base-10 counting (numbers near 10 and 20)
  • Some researchers believe it's the world's first mathematical sieve for finding primes!

Belgian anthropologist Jean de Heinzelin de Braucourt, who discovered the bone, noted that the markings were "deliberately planned" - not random scratches but organized mathematical thinking from our African ancestors in the Stone Age.

Before the Ishango: The Lebombo Bone

Even older is the Lebombo bone from the border of South Africa and Swaziland, dating to around 35,000-43,000 years ago. It has 29 notches carved in a row - potentially the earliest known tally stick, predating written language by about 28,000 years!

This means humans were thinking about numbers - including counting in odd and even quantities - for tens of thousands of years before we invented writing.

Mesopotamia: Where Odd Met Even (Officially)

Fast forward to around 3000-2000 BC, and the Sumerians and Babylonians in ancient Mesopotamia (modern-day Iraq) developed sophisticated number systems carved into clay tablets. With hundreds of tablets surviving, we know they:

  • Used a sexagesimal (base-60) system that could distinguish odd from even
  • Created multiplication tables that showed odd √ó odd = odd

  • Left us the legacy of 60 seconds, 60 minutes, and 360 degrees in a circle

The Babylonians didn't just count - they did algebra, geometry, and even calculated square roots. A tablet from 1600 BC shows ‚àö2 calculated to 7 decimal places!

Ancient Egypt: Odd Hieroglyphs

The Egyptians (from before 3000 BC) used hieroglyphic symbols for different powers of 10. While they didn't have a specific symbol for "odd," their mathematical papyri show they understood that numbers ending in 1, 3, 5, 7, or 9 behaved differently than those ending in 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8.

The Rhind Mathematical Papyrus (c. 1650 BC) contains problems that implicitly use odd and even number properties, particularly in fraction calculations.

The Ancient Odd Squad

Pythagoras: The OG Odd Believer

Around 500 BC, Pythagoras and his followers didn't just discover that whole triangle thing. They also developed a philosophical system where numbers had mystical properties. Odd numbers were considered "unlimited" and even numbers "limited," because odd numbers cannot be divided into two equal parts - they always have that extra unit that refuses to be paired.

The Pythagoreans believed that understanding numbers was the key to understanding the universe itself. They saw mathematical patterns everywhere in nature and considered number theory a path to enlightenment! ‚ú®

Theorems That'll Make You Odd-ly Happy

Goldbach's Conjecture: 282 Years and Still Unsolved

In 1742, Christian Goldbach wrote to Euler proposing that every even number greater than 2 is the sum of two prime numbers. For example:

  • 4 = 2 + 2
  • 6 = 3 + 3
  • 8 = 3 + 5
  • 100 = 47 + 53 (or 41 + 59, or 3 + 97...)

The twist? It's been verified up to 4 √ó 1018 but never proven for ALL even numbers. The "weak" version (every odd > 5 is the sum of three primes) was only proven in 2013 by Harald Helfgott!

Fermat's Last Theorem: Odd Primes Only Club

Pierre de Fermat claimed (in a margin, because of course) that xn + yn = zn has no positive integer solutions for n > 2.

The kicker? To prove it for all n, you only need to prove it for odd primes! Why? Because every integer greater than 2 is either divisible by 4 or by an odd prime. Fermat himself proved n=4, so mathematicians spent 358 years working on odd prime cases until Andrew Wiles cracked it in 1995.

Sophie Germain (the only woman in our odd tale) proved the first case for all primes less than 270. Not bad for someone who had to pretend to be a man to study math! üí™

The Feit-Thompson Theorem: Odd Groups Are Solvable

This 1963 theorem states that every finite group of odd order is solvable. The proof? A casual 255 pages. The implication? Deep connections between odd numbers and abstract algebra that most mathematicians still don't fully understand.

It's proof that odd numbers aren't just for counting - they're fundamental to the structure of mathematics itself!

Odd Perfect Numbers: Math's Bigfoot

A perfect number equals the sum of its proper divisors. For example, 6 = 1 + 2 + 3. We know the formula for even perfect numbers (discovered by Euclid around 300 BC), but odd perfect numbers?

Status: Unknown for 2,300+ years ü§∑

What we DO know:

  • If one exists, it's bigger than 102,200 (that's a number with 2,200 digits!)
  • Euler proved it must be a prime raised to an odd power times a square
  • Descartes found "spoofs" - numbers that are almost odd perfect numbers
  • The Great Internet Mersenne Prime Search (GIMPS) continues the hunt

They're the Sasquatch of mathematics - rumored, elusive, and stubbornly resistant to selfies. üì∏

The Babylonian Legacy

The ancient Babylonians (around 1800 BC) had clay tablets with tables of odd and even numbers! Their sexagesimal (base-60) system was so sophisticated that they could distinguish between numbers divisible by 2 and those that weren't - crucial for their complex calculations involving fractions and astronomy.

This legacy lives on today: we still use 60 seconds in a minute and 60 minutes in an hour, thanks to the Babylonians' love of highly divisible numbers!

All Primes Are Odd (Except One!)

Why Odd Numbers Matter

Beyond their quirky properties, odd numbers are fundamental to:

Cryptography

RSA encryption (the tech securing your online banking) relies on the difficulty of factoring large numbers into odd primes. Every time you shop online, you're trusting in the oddness!

Quantum Mechanics

Fermions (particles like electrons) obey Fermi-Dirac statistics, which depend on odd/even symmetry properties. Literally, the universe cares about oddness at the quantum level!

Symmetry & Beauty

From five-petaled flowers to the three-fold symmetry of certain crystals, odd numbers appear throughout nature. The Fibonacci sequence (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21...) contains infinitely many odds, creating the golden ratio that defines beauty itself!
